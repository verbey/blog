How did I get into studying Japanese

こんにちは! So, this was the situation half a year ago:

The university made us choose 2 additional subjects from around 30. Most of them were useless, like business planning, which was one of the two subjects that I chose (you know, the type of subject where you will be told that you are poor because you have the wrong way of thinking).

Among one of them, a few language subjects stood out: Italian, Chinese and Japanese.

I know my university had cultural exchanges with people from the respective countries, so I knew I would learn at least something there.

But I could only choose one, so I had to make my choice wisely.

Italian language was taught at the computer science institute, so it was the first one to go as an option. By the way, before you ask the question, I will tell you the answer: “I don’t know why. What I do know, though, is that study at a very cursed place.”

Chinese is a very popular language, for obvious reasons. Why learn Japanese when there will be more uses for Chinese?

Well, because I have been told a story that my university has some good Japanese teachers. In particular, I’ve heard that one of the professors here lived for some time on the Kuril Islands, where he was taught Japanese by native speakers.

I don’t know if any of this is true because it was passed on to me as gossip, but the old man does have a Japanese state award and even has a Wikipedia article about himself. No ordinary person can have that.

Also, I happened to have been exposed to Japanese media. This influenced my decision as well. Just a little. I promise.

Considering this, I have made the decision to study Japanese.

First impression

It’s crazy. Being a European, naturally, I find it hard.

The hardest thing, in my opinion, is Kanji. For those who don’t know, Japanese has 3 writing systems: Hiragana, Katakana, and Kanji. Despite all of them being based on the Chinese hieroglyphs, the kanas only have symbols for representing a fairly limited set of sounds.

Kanji (translated as Chinese letters), on the other hand, consists of thousands of hyeroglyphs, which could even have different readings. Scary, isn’t it?

Also, when a European person begins learning the language, they are immediately struck with massive cultural differences.

Showing your respect is very important. Doing so is a thing in all cultures, I think, but the Japanese will be going an extre mile with that one.

Also, when learning about modern Japan you can’t help but run into the culture of overworking.

Wealth of Japan, its national capital, is the product of an immense sacrifice of its workers. Billions of working hours put to the altar by millions of workers.

Lessons

In our lessons, we use an online textbook as our main resource.

Marugoto is an excellent all-in-one resource. Our teacher only works as a mentor, answers advanced questions, helps with pronunciation etc.

This is actually very good because it makes studying more or less self-paced. If you want to, you can rush and go through all the topics in a week. If you missed some concept you can always revisit it later.

Tasks in the A1 section are dead simple and repetitive, yet it’s all you need at this point of learning — get a grasp on some of the most basic communication. More understanding as to why the language works the way it does will come later.

Exam

Have you ever had your mark be lowered because of JoJo? No?

What a bizarre question, right? Well, I did.

For the exam, we had to do a test on the Marugoto platform and write a short text about ourselves.

So… I thought it would be a good idea to start the text with an edited version of the Kira Yoshikage copypasta.

Well, as it turned out, the teacher (せんせい, sensei), made us read aloud the first two sentences and judged the final grade on them, whilst ignoring the rest of the text. That was most unfortunate because the two sentences that the text started with, were the pasta, written without due respect one should’ve shown in a more formal environment.

Long story short: JoJo ruins people’s lives. And grades.

Thankfully, I still got a good grade - 80%. I would have lost my mind if Japanese was yet another subject for which I had to work my ass off to get a pitiful grade.

Conclusion

Japanese is an interesting language and I genuinely like it despite the fact of how hard it is. I might not have as much free time nowadays as I used to in the past, but it gives me joy to spend it on studying it.

I might not be well-versed in it and can only understand some basic phrases, but look forward to reaching new heights and feel very proud of myself each time I can spot something in Japanese in the wild and read or translate it.

Oh, I almost forgot that one. Setting up the input for Japanese on KDE Plasma sucks. One R*ddit commenter suggested this might be the reason the Japanese don’t use Linux much.

As a cherry on the top of the post, I would like to offer you this hot take: hiragana looks better than katakana.

Bonus: Useful resources for self-studying

These are the resources I stumbled upon during my studies and highly recommend to anybody who would like to learn the language as well:

Marugoto - self-paced free course for basic Japanese.
Nihonoari - useful app for studying Hiragana and Katakana. With that one, I memorised most of the characters from both alphabets in a matter of days!
Usagi-chan’s hiragana and katakana challenges - another useful resources for memorising the kana.
r/learnjapanese’s wiki - good meta resource for finding other resources. Unfortunately, hosted on Reddit.
Tofugu - a neat blog with articles answering questions you might have and some good visuals.
